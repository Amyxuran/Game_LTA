:Twenty years earlier. A cramped university laboratory. Flickering fluorescent lights. Papers and petri dishes piled everywhere. A young Philip Morrison, thirty-two years old, dark-haired, with a nearly obsessive kind of light in his eyes.;

:He studied dopamine circuitry because of his sister, Margaret.;

:Margaret suffered from severe generalized anxiety disorder. She was a gifted architect, but anxiety held her throat in an invisible fist. She couldn't finish projects. She couldn't leave the house. Eventually she couldn't even get out of bed. The medications available at the time, SSRIs and benzodiazepines, either carried unbearable side effects or merely smothered the symptoms like bandages over a wound.;

:Philip wanted a true solution. Not symptom suppression. Direct modulation of the circuit itself.;

:The discovery was accidental. He was testing a batch of rare fruit extracts imported from South America when one tropical berry compound unexpectedly showed an extremely high affinity for dopamine D2 receptors. In low doses, it could temporarily quiet anxiety and induce a subjective state resembling meditation.;

:He tested it on Margaret. The result surpassed all expectation. Within three days she walked out of her room for the first time, stood in the yard, looked up at the sky, and said, "The sky is so blue." It was the first sentence she'd spoken in two years that had nothing to do with fear.;

:Philip was so exhilarated he didn't sleep. He wrote the paper and published it in Frontiers in Neuroscience. That paper changed everything.;

:First came the media. Then pharmaceutical companies. Finally the military.;

:Two men in dark suits appeared in his lab. They didn't need to introduce themselves. Their posture and their eyes made their purpose obvious. They said they were very interested in the treatment's potential for PTSD. For traumatized veterans. A social good, wasn't it?;

:Philip agreed. He thought he was doing the right thing.;

:Three months later, at a classified meeting, he saw the real project brief. They didn't want to treat trauma. They wanted to know what would happen if high-achieving people lost their drive to compete. Could the compound be weaponized? If enemy scientists, engineers, commanders all lost ambition after consuming it, would they still be effective?;

:Philip slammed his hand on the table. He said no.;

:They said fine. Then they withdrew every dollar of support, buried the paper, erased two years of research records. Overnight he went from rising academic star to a nobody with a blank stretch in his CV.;

:It took him five years to rebuild his career from the ground up. He buried everything about EDEN as deeply as he could, as if burying a corpse.;
