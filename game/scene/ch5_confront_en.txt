:Twenty years ago. A cramped university lab, fluorescent lights flickering, the desk buried under papers and petri dishes. A young Philip Morrison, thirty-two, dark-haired, with a light in his eyes that bordered on obsession.;

:He wasn't working for the military. He wasn't chasing academic prestige. He was studying dopamine circuits because of his sister — Margaret.;

:Margaret had severe generalized anxiety disorder. She was a brilliant architect, but anxiety was an invisible hand around her throat, squeezing every hour of every day. She couldn't finish projects, couldn't leave the house, and eventually couldn't get out of bed. The medications available then — SSRIs, benzodiazepines — either came with devastating side effects or merely suppressed the symptoms, like putting a Band-Aid over a wound.;

:Philip wanted a real solution. Not symptom suppression — a reset of the circuit itself.;

:The discovery was accidental. He was testing a batch of rare fruit extracts imported from South America. A compound in one tropical berry showed an unexpected, remarkable affinity for the dopamine D2 receptor. At low doses, it could temporarily quiet anxiety and bring the brain into a state resembling deep meditation. Not numbness — clarity.;

:He tested it on Margaret. The results exceeded anything he'd imagined. Within three days, she walked out of her room for the first time in months, stood in the yard, looked up at the sky, and said: "The sky is so blue." It was the first sentence she'd spoken in two years that had nothing to do with fear.;

:Philip stayed up all night, electrified. He wrote the paper and published it in Frontiers in Neuroscience. That paper changed everything.;

:First came the media coverage. Then the calls from pharmaceutical companies. And finally — the military.;

:Two men in dark suits appeared in his lab. They didn't need to introduce themselves; their bearing said everything. They expressed "great interest" in PTSD treatment. Post-traumatic stress in veterans was a massive social problem, wasn't it?;

:Philip agreed. He believed he was doing good.;

:But three months later, at a classified briefing, he saw the real project proposal. They didn't just want to treat trauma — they wanted to know what happens when high-achievers lose their competitive drive. Could the compound be weaponized? If enemy scientists, engineers, and military commanders all ingested it, would they still be capable of fighting?;

:Philip slammed his fist on the table. He said no.;

:They said fine. Then they pulled all his funding, retracted his paper, and erased two years of research records. Overnight, he went from rising academic star to a nameless researcher with an unexplained gap on his CV.;

:It took him five years to rebuild his career. From scratch. Starting as a teaching assistant. Starting by grading undergrad homework. He buried everything about EDEN in the deepest corner of his memory, like burying a body.;
