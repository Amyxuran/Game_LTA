:Two weeks later, Anna presents her new proposal to the committee. Prof. Zhang questions the methodology's feasibility. Dr. Roberts raises concerns about sample size. Philip asks only one question: "Are you sure this is what you want to do?";

:The committee conditionally approves the new proposal — she has a year to complete data collection and write the dissertation.;

:Over the next year, Anna becomes a different person. Not the apple kind of "different person" — she's still anxious, still loses sleep, still doubts herself at three in the morning. But this time, every time she sits back down at her laptop, she knows why she's writing.;

:Philip meets with her biweekly to review chapter drafts. His red pen is still sharp, but the nature of his comments has changed — no longer "wrong direction," but "this argument could be tighter.";

:The final dissertation is submitted to the full committee three weeks before the defense. Per university policy, each member must read the complete manuscript and submit written feedback. Philip's written response is a single line: "Ready to defend.";

:Defense day.;

:Fourth floor, Room 406. The fluorescent tubes on the ceiling emit a faint hum, like background music without a melody. The room is modest — three rows of seats hold a scattered audience of maybe fifteen people. A handful of fellow grad students, two undergrads sitting in, and one person who clearly wandered into the wrong room but decided to stay for the show.;

:Three people sit at the committee's long table. Prof. Zhang on the left, Dr. Roberts in the center, Philip on the right. Their printed copies of Anna's dissertation are dog-eared, margins dense with annotations.;

:Anna stands at the podium. The projector casts her thesis title on the screen behind her: "Redefining Success: Autonomy, Meaning, and the Pressure of Achievement.";

:Her fingers tremble slightly. Not from fear — from caring. This is the first time she's stood on a stage for something she truly believes in.;

Anna:Our society has constructed a narrative about success. This narrative tells us: suffering has value because it leads to achievement. Rest is shameful because others are still running. Giving up is cowardice, even when you're being crushed.;

Anna:This thesis is not about denying the value of effort. It asks a more fundamental question: when we bind the dopamine reward system to social evaluation, can we still tell the difference between genuine passion and programmed desire?;

:She clicks to the next slide. A set of data appears — anxiety and depression rates among graduate students. The numbers are staggering.;

Anna:We're not discussing a hypothesis. These numbers are real. Behind every percentage point is a living person, staring at the ceiling at three a.m., questioning whether their existence has meaning.;

:She pauses. Her gaze sweeps across the audience, then lands on Philip.;

:His expression is unreadable. Not cold, not approving. More like someone catching a reflection in a mirror they'd rather not face.;

Anna:My conclusion is simple. Success shouldn't have only one definition. And our education system needs to give students the space to redefine it — not as a refuge for the defeated, but as a choice for the awakened.;

:She closes her notebook. The projector's beam falls on her like a solitary spotlight.;

Anna:That concludes my presentation. Thank you.;

:Silence.;

:The kind of silence that could tip either way. It could be the silence of "we need to seriously discuss this," or the silence of "is this student joking?" Anna feels like her heartbeat is loud enough for the entire room to hear.;

:Philip is the first to speak.;
