:Two weeks later, Anna stands before the committee and presents the new direction. Professor Zhang questions the methodological feasibility. Dr. Roberts worries about the sample size. Philip asks only one thing: "Are you sure this is what you want to do?" ;

:The committee conditionally approves the proposal, requiring her to finish data collection and the dissertation within a year.;

:For the next year and more, Anna works like a different person. Not the way the apple changes a person. She is still anxious. She still lies awake at night. She still doubts herself at three in the morning. But every time she sits down again, she knows why she is writing.;

:Philip meets with her every two weeks to review chapters. His red-ink comments are still sharp, but the nature of them has changed. No longer "the direction is wrong," now "this argument could be more rigorous." ;

:The final full dissertation is submitted to the entire committee three weeks before the defense. By protocol, they all have to read it beforehand. Philip's written feedback is one sentence long: "You may proceed to defense." ;

:Defense day.;

:Room 406, fourth floor. Fluorescent tubes hum overhead with the kind of sound that never becomes music. The room is small. A little over a dozen people are scattered in the audience rows, a few graduate students from the department, two undergraduates sitting in out of curiosity, and someone who clearly wandered into the wrong room but decided to stay and watch.;

:Three committee members sit behind the long table. Professor Zhang on the left, Dr. Roberts in the center, Philip on the right. The printed copies of her dissertation are worn at the corners now, margins crowded with notes.;

changeFigure:char-anna.png -next;

:Anna stands at the lectern with the title of her dissertation projected behind her: "When We Redefine Success: Autonomy, Meaning, and Achievement Pressure." ;

:Her fingers tremble slightly. Not from fear. From caring. This is the first time she has stood in front of a room for something she truly believes in.;

Anna:Our society has constructed a narrative about success. That narrative tells us pain is valuable because it leads to achievement. Rest is shameful because someone else is still running. Giving up is cowardice, even if you are being crushed while you persist.;

Anna:This dissertation is not an attempt to deny the value of effort. It asks a more fundamental question: when dopamine circuits associated with reward become entangled with social evaluation, can we still distinguish true passion from programmed desire?;

:She switches slides. A set of statistics appears, anxiety and depression rates among graduate students. The numbers are brutal.;

Anna:We are not discussing abstractions. These numbers are real. Every single percentage point represents a real person staring at a ceiling at midnight and wondering what the point of continuing is.;

:She pauses and lets her gaze pass over the audience before settling on Philip.;

:His expression is unreadable. Not cold, not approving. More like a man seeing in a mirror a reflection he has spent years trying not to look at.;

Anna:My conclusion is simple. Success should not have only one definition. And our educational system has to make room for students to redefine it, not as a retreat for failures, but as a choice made by people who have finally woken up.;

:She closes the laptop. The projector beam strikes across her body like a lonely spotlight.;

Anna:That concludes my presentation. Thank you.;

:Silence.;

:The kind of silence that can fall either way. The kind that might mean we need to think carefully, or might mean this student has gone completely mad. Anna can hear her own pulse in her ears.;
